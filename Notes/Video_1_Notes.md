## 🎲 Vibe Coding — What It Is

**Vibe coding** is a modern software development approach where a person relies heavily on AI — specifically LLMs — to generate, refine, and debug code based on natural language prompts.

### The 3 steps of vibe coding:
```
Plain English Prompt → AI Generates Code → Iterative Refinement (repeat until you get what you want)
```

> **Analogy:** It's like texting a very capable assistant and saying "make me a website" — they build something, you say "change this, fix that" — back and forth until it looks right. Fast, but you're not fully in control.

---

## ✅ When Vibe Coding is Fine

Vibe coding works well for **low stakes projects:**
- Minimum Viable Products (MVPs) — quick prototypes to test an idea
- Exploring and experimenting with ideas
- Hackathons — where speed matters more than quality

> **What is an MVP?** A Minimum Viable Product is the simplest version of something that still works — just enough to test whether an idea is worth building properly.

---

## ❌ When Vibe Coding is NOT Enough

Vibe coding breaks down for **high stakes projects:**
- Scalable systems — websites used by crores of people
- Critical infrastructure — banking, healthcare, payments
- Anything with a lot of money or user data on the line

> **Why?** Because when AI makes all the decisions, you don't fully understand what was built or why. In high stakes situations, that lack of understanding is dangerous — bugs are costly, security gaps are exploited, and fixing things takes much longer than building them right the first time.

💡 **Job Tip:** Most real companies fall into the high stakes category. Knowing *when not to* vibe code is just as important as knowing how to use AI effectively.

## 🧠 Concept: What is an LLM?

**LLM** stands for **Large Language Model** — the type of AI that powers Claude, ChatGPT, and similar tools.

It's trained on huge amounts of text and code, which is why it can understand natural language and write code at the same time.

> **Analogy:** An LLM is like someone who has read millions of books, articles, and code repositories. They haven't *built* everything they've read about — but they understand patterns so well they can produce very convincing output.
